The two-phase structure is the key message on this slide. Phase 1 answers "does the signal work in ideal conditions?" — Phase 2 answers "how much can we trust it when sensors degrade?"

PHASE 1 FRAMING FOR DR. PISU:

The two-row structure of existing data (recovery vs. noescape with identical inputs) is
the critical empirical setup. Stress this: these are the SAME scenario from the
framework's input perspective. Same road, same obstacle distance, same speed. The ONLY
difference is a single Autoware config parameter (avoidance policy = manual vs. auto).
The framework's job is to output "avoidance warranted" for both — the comparison of
outcomes proves that acting on that output makes a difference (vehicle avoids vs. stays
stuck). This is how we isolate the framework's contribution from confounding variables.

The two needed conditions (obs_singlelane, obs_tooclosetoreact) complete the validation
by providing the "MRC mandatory" ground truth cases. Without them, we can validate
"avoidance warranted" detection but not the rejection criterion. Framing them as
"planned collection on the new machine" rather than "missing data" shows the research
design is complete — execution is just pending hardware access.

Route diversity note: the committee will likely ask whether the results generalize
beyond three similar Shinjuku routes. The honest answer is: not yet validated. New
routes should be selected to include topological variety (single-lane sections, different
intersection types, busier traffic patterns) before claiming generalization.

PHASE 2 FRAMING FOR DR. PISU:

The confidence function C(fault_type, magnitude) is what elevates this from a binary
classifier to a reliability-aware decision system. Dr. Pisu's controls background will
recognize this as analogous to sensor fusion with uncertain measurements — the system
knows "how much to trust" each signal given current sensor health.

Camera fault → traffic light recognition: If the camera is degraded, the model may not
know that the traffic light is red, and therefore predicts the vehicle will NOT decelerate
— but the vehicle does decelerate (because Autoware has other ways to detect red lights,
or the light was detected intermittently). This would produce a false NLL spike. The
confidence function shows how often this happens at each fault magnitude.

IMU fault → ego state estimate: IMU noise propagates through the localization EKF into
the kinematic_state topic, which feeds the ST-GAT's position/velocity/heading inputs.
Degraded inputs produce noisier residuals, which may either suppress real spikes (false
negatives) or create spurious ones (false positives). The confidence function captures
this degradation empirically.

The Phase 2 conditions are chosen to be minimal but sufficient: two fault types × two
magnitudes × two base scenarios = 8 conditions. Each condition gets ~12 runs. This is
approximately 4 days of data collection on the new machine — realistic within a semester.

ANTICIPATED QUESTION — "Why not test obs_singlelane and obs_tooclosetoreact under faults
as well?"

Answer: That would add 8 more conditions (16 more if two magnitudes). The marginal value
is low — we already know Signal 1 and Signal 2 are deterministic (topology from HD map,
kinematics from planning). Sensor faults that matter most are those that affect Signal 3
(the behavioral residual), because that is the only stochastic component. Topology lookup
from the map is not affected by camera or IMU faults; TTC from planned trajectory is
minimally affected. This is why fault experiments focus on the obs_recovery base scenario.
