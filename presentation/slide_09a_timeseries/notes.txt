WHAT THE PLOT IS SHOWING

The y-axis is the digital twin's "surprise score" — formally the Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) of the
vehicle's actual behavior under the model's prediction. Think of it this way: the digital twin has
learned what normal Autoware driving looks like. Every tenth of a second, it asks "how surprising
is what the vehicle just did?" A very negative number (−12, −14) means "exactly what I expected —
perfectly normal driving." A number near zero or positive means "this vehicle is doing something I
have never seen before — something is wrong."

The x-axis is normalized run time — 0 is the moment the vehicle starts, 1 is the moment the run
ends (either because the vehicle reached its goal, or because it got stuck behind the obstacle and
couldn't move). The normalization is necessary because recovery runs vary widely in length
(42 to 1622 windows) while noescape runs are shorter and more uniform (~274 windows). Without
normalizing, you cannot average across runs — a 162-second run and a 4-second run would have their
avoidance events at completely different x positions. By stretching every run to [0, 1], we align
all runs so that "the avoidance event" and "the stuck event" land at roughly the same normalized
time across runs.

Each line is the average surprise score across all 18 runs for that condition, taken at each point
in normalized time. The shaded band is the spread (±1 standard deviation) — wider band means runs
vary more from each other at that moment.

GUIDING THE INTERPRETATION

Start at the left side of the plot (x = 0). Both lines start at similar values, around −11 to −12.
This means at the beginning of every run, the vehicle is driving normally and the digital twin is
not surprised. So far, both conditions look identical — the vehicle has not reached the obstacle yet.

Now look at the blue line (obs_recovery, avoidance enabled). Around x = 0.25, there is a clear bump
upward — the surprise score rises from −12 toward −9 or so before dropping back down. This bump is
the avoidance maneuver: at this moment, the vehicle is executing a lane change to go around the
obstacle. The digital twin was never shown this behavior at this location during training, so its
surprise score spikes. After the maneuver (x > 0.35), the vehicle is past the obstacle and driving
normally again — the surprise score drops back to −12 and stays there for the rest of the run.

Now look at the orange line (obs_noescape, avoidance disabled). It stays flat and low (around −12
to −14) for the entire run. The vehicle approaches the obstacle, brakes smoothly, and waits. Smooth
braking is completely normal — the digital twin has seen that thousands of times. No surprise. No
spike. The run ends (x = 1) because the vehicle is stuck, not because anything anomalous happened
from the model's perspective.

The gray dashed line (−6.37) is the anomaly threshold. A run that crosses this line is classified
as anomalous by the framework. Notice that the averaged blue line barely reaches −9 at its peak —
it does not cross the threshold on average. This is important to explain correctly (see below).

WHAT THE PLOT LACKS AND WHY IT'S OKAY

The averaged blue line does not cross the threshold, even though individual recovery runs do (as
shown in the box plot on the next slide). This is a consequence of the averaging: the avoidance
maneuver happens at slightly different normalized times across the 18 runs. When you average 18
spikes that are slightly offset from each other, the average peak is lower than any individual
spike. The signal is real — it just gets smoothed out by the averaging.

Think of it like this: if 18 people all clap their hands but not quite at the same instant, the
average sound level stays low even though each individual clap is loud. The box plot (slide 09b)
is the "did any individual run produce a loud clap?" question — and the answer there is clearly yes.

WHAT THE NEW DATA WILL ADD

This plot only tests Signal 3 (behavioral residual) because both conditions are on the same
multi-lane roads with the obstacle at the same distance. The new data collection adds two conditions
that directly test the other two signals:

  obs_singlelane (~12 runs): obstacle placed in a single-lane section of the Shinjuku route.
    When the vehicle approaches, there is no adjacent lane — avoidance is geometrically impossible.
    Expected result: Autoware stops (or AEB fires). The digital twin will still see the same
    braking behavior, but Signal 1 (topology check) will now fire "no adjacent lane" and correctly
    prevent the framework from declaring avoidance warranted. If this works, we expect the orange
    (noescape) and singlelane lines to look similar on this plot — both flat — because in both
    cases the vehicle brakes and stops without any anomalous maneuver.

  obs_tooclosetoreact (~12 runs): obstacle placed 5–8 m ahead (vs. 30 m in current data).
    At this distance, TTC is less than 1 second — physically impossible to execute a lane change
    safely even if one is geometrically available. Expected result: AEB fires immediately.
    Signal 2 (kinematic TTC check) should fire and prevent avoidance from being declared.
    On this plot, the surprise score may actually spike (because AEB braking is aggressive and
    unexpected) but the framework should correctly label it "MRC mandatory" not "avoidance warranted"
    — because Signal 2 already blocked the avoidance output before Signal 3 was evaluated.

With all four conditions in one plot, the story becomes clearer: the surprise score spike is
necessary but not sufficient. The topology and kinematic signals tell you when the spike should
trigger avoidance vs. when it should trigger a conservative stop. That is the three-signal
AND-gate in action.
