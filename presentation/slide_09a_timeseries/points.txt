RESULT 1: POPULATION-LEVEL NLL TIME-SERIES
(Plot: nll_timeseries.png)

Setup:
  • 18 runs per condition (obs_recovery, obs_noescape)
  • Each run normalized to [0, 1] time axis so runs of different lengths can be averaged
  • 30-window moving average applied before averaging (smooths window-to-window noise)
  • Shaded band = ±1 SD across runs — shows run-to-run variability, not measurement noise

What to read:
  • Blue (recovery):  mean near nominal → brief SPIKE at ~25% through run → returns to nominal
    — spike = avoidance maneuver (lane change); NLL crosses 3σ threshold
    — spike at ~25% because vehicle encounters obstacle, avoids, then drives ~75% more of route
    — wide SD band at spike = avoidance happens at slightly different normalized times across runs
  • Orange (noescape): mean flat throughout, never crosses threshold
    — vehicle brakes and waits every run; no unexpected maneuver; digital twin is never surprised

Key message:
  The spike is brief (~2–3 s out of a 60–160 s run).
  Full-run CVaR averages over all the nominal driving time and HIDES the spike.
  → Max NLL (or short-window CVaR) is the right statistic for classification — shown next.

Note on the early variance in recovery:
  Wide band at t=0 is an artifact of one anomalously short run (42 windows) stretching to fill [0,1].
  It does not indicate early anomalies — it reflects the normalization of a run that ended quickly.
