Dr. Pisu's background in risk assessment means he will be comfortable with NLL as a probabilistic scoring rule. The key intuition to convey: a well-trained model on nominal data assigns HIGH likelihood (low NLL, negative values) to nominal trajectories. When something non-nominal happens — an avoidance lane change, an emergency brake — the actual observation falls in the tail of the predicted distribution, causing NLL to spike upward (less negative, or positive). This is the same principle as a Kalman filter innovation sequence — if the innovation exceeds the expected bounds, something outside the model assumptions is happening.

The σ² in the output is crucial and worth spending time on: because the model outputs full Gaussian parameters rather than point estimates, a situation where the model is uncertain (high σ²) is treated differently from one where the model is confident but wrong. This prevents false alarms from high-variance nominal situations. CVaR further concentrates on the tail rather than the mean, making it robust to brief nominal interruptions within otherwise anomalous windows.

The negative NLL values are a common point of confusion. Explain: for a multivariate Gaussian in well-calibrated nominal conditions, the log-likelihood is negative (the density integrates to 1, so log-density values are typically below 0). The model achieving val NLL = -513.7 means it assigns very high probability to the actual next state — it has learned the distribution well.
