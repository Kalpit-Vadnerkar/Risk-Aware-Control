The three-panel layout is intentional: it shows one positive result and two null results, and the null results are informative rather than failures.

The middle panel (CVaR full run) is a statistical lesson in signal design. The spike in recovery runs lasts approximately 25 windows (~2.5 seconds) out of a run averaging 800+ windows. CVaR₉₅ averages the worst 5% of windows — at 800 windows, that is 40 windows. The spike windows are included, but so are 15 other high-NLL windows from ordinary acceleration events. The result is that CVaR for recovery is barely different from CVaR for noescape. This is not a failure of the residual signal — it is a failure of the statistic. Max NLL, or a windowed CVaR (rolling 50-window CVaR), would recover the separation.

The right panel (has_adjacent_lane) shows a null result that supports the research design. Both conditions place the obstacle on the same multi-lane roads, so of course the topology signal shows no difference. The panel is in the presentation because it demonstrates that we have implemented the topology feature correctly and tested it, not because we expect it to separate these two conditions. The result is: "this signal is ready, but needs the right dataset." Dr. Pisu should see this as evidence that the research plan is systematic, not as a failed signal.

The Mann-Whitney test is appropriate here because: (a) we cannot assume normality with n=18, (b) the distributions are bounded and skewed, (c) it is consistent with prior AV safety literature that uses non-parametric tests for small simulation samples. If asked why not t-test: the NLL distribution has a heavy tail (driven by the spike windows) and t-test would be invalid.
