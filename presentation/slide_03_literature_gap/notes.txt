The key point for Dr. Pisu: risk assessment tells you HOW risky a situation is, and avoidance planning tells you HOW to avoid — but nothing tells you WHEN avoidance is the appropriate response vs. stopping. This is the decision-theory layer that sits between risk quantification and action execution. It is analogous to the mode-switching logic in hybrid systems control, which is well-studied in controls theory but not in the AV safety context.

Be prepared for the question: "Isn't this just threshold-based TTC with a lane check?"

The answer is no — and this is worth defending carefully because it is the core justification for Signal 3.

Consider the false-positive problem: a vehicle decelerates toward a red light at 25m ahead, at 6 m/s. TTC = 25/6 ≈ 4.2 s. The road is two-lane. Signal 1 (topology) passes. Signal 2 (kinematic) passes if the TTC threshold is τ_min = 3 s. A pure TTC + lane-check system would output "avoidance warranted" — and Autoware would attempt a lane change at an intersection, which is dangerous. This would fire on every red light, crosswalk stop, or slow vehicle on a multi-lane road.

The fundamental problem is that "decelerating toward something 30m ahead on a multi-lane road" is kinematically and topologically identical whether that something is a red light or a blocked lane. The trajectory alone cannot distinguish them. Distinguishing them requires either (a) semantic scene understanding — classify what's ahead — which depends on the quality of the perception pipeline and fails under occlusion; or (b) a model of nominal behavior that flags when the vehicle's response is unexpected. We use (b).

The digital twin was trained on nominal driving, which includes stopping at signals, yielding at crosswalks, and following slow vehicles. When the vehicle performs a planned stop, the model predicts smooth deceleration and NLL stays low — the stop is in the model's distribution. When an unexpected obstacle blocks the lane and the vehicle executes an avoidance maneuver (lateral lane change, off-trajectory swerve), the model has no prior for that behavior at that point in the route. NLL spikes sharply — precisely because the vehicle is doing something it has never done at that location in training.

This is the only real-time evidence that distinguishes "unexpected blockage requiring a decision" from "planned stop." Without Signal 3, Signals 1 and 2 produce an unacceptable false-positive rate on ordinary urban driving. Signal 3 is the gating condition that makes the system safe to activate.

Additional nuance: lowering the TTC threshold does not solve this — it just delays the false positive until the vehicle is dangerously close. And raising it misses real fast-approaching obstacles. The residual signal has no such trade-off: it fires based on the vehicle's behavioral deviation, not on distance or time alone.
